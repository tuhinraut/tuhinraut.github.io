Dogma as Grandma

In this blog, we will draw analogies between our grandma's secret recipes and the process of protein synthesis and cell's functioning through the central dogma of molecular biology, i.e. DNA --> RNA --> Protein. We will also explore topics far beyond just the simple central dogma and explore analogies between the genome, plasmids, protein folding machineries and many other concepts. But had to keep the title "Dogma as Grandma" because how could I not.... .

central dogma grandma cooking analogies

🍳 Grandma's Cookbook (DNA)

You know that feeling when you smell your grandma's cooking from three blocks away and suddenly you're 8 years old again, practically floating toward the kitchen like a cartoon character? Let's say you have such a super grandma (which you do, ik) and she makes the world's best food. Like the stuff even Gordon Ramsay would lick the plate off of. When once you pleaded with her to reveal her secrets, she pulls out her years old cookbook binder that had been passed down to her. The recipes have the instructions regarding what all ingredients are required, how to mix those and prepare the dish. Your eyes light up on seeing the holy grail and you reach out to grab the book and go to town with it, but as it is the only copy of the recipes she has, she slaps your hand lightly and says you can't have it, yet. She knows you too well and how careless you can be :'(.

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

DNA is the master cookbook that contains all recipes needed to build and maintain life, but it's too precious to be directly used for cooking.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Grandma's precious cookbook represents the genome - the complete set of genetic instructions passed down through generations
  • Individual recipes represent genes - specific instructions for making particular proteins
  • Recipe ingredients represent nucleotide bases - Adenine, Thymine, Guanine, and Cytosine (ATGC), the ABCs of DNA
  • Protective behavior represents DNA's nuclear protection - safely stored in the cell nucleus, never directly exposed

Key Process: Just as grandma won't let you take her original cookbook to the kitchen, DNA never leaves the nucleus. Instead, the cell makes temporary copies (mRNA) of specific recipes (genes) when proteins need to be made, ensuring the master instructions stay protected.

Fun Fact: A single human cell's DNA contains about 3 billion base pairs of information - that's equivalent to storing about 750 MB of data, roughly the same as a CD-ROM (for all you millennials out there) full of recipes!

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

mRNA is like temporary recipe copies with built-in expiration dates and special encoding for security and cellular control.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Jotting down on paper represents transcription - encrypt DNA into mRNA
  • 3-hour shredding deadline represents mRNA degradation - temporary nature for gene regulation
  • GenZ acronyms represents genetic code changes - DNA's T (thymine) becomes RNA's U (uracil)

Key Process: RNA polymerase enzymes copy specific gene sections into mRNA molecules that are designed to self-destruct after a short time. This is balanced by the presence of a poly-A tail, to delay the destruction of main content of mRNA. This temporary nature allows cells to quickly ramp up or shut down protein production based on changing needs.

Fun Fact: Some mRNA molecules last only 2 minutes, while others can survive for days - it depends on how tightly the cell needs to control that particular protein's production!

👨‍🍳 Let Him COOK! (Protein Synthesis)

Now you run off to your local shop and hand the shopkeeper over the list of ingredients (Thankfully he understood your slangs). Going down the list, he gives you all of the ingredients. You pay him and dash back home to marinate the chicken you just bought (you are under a time limit, remember!). You follow the recipe to a T, and add all the ingredients one after another according to script. Then you cover it with a loin cloth and stow it away for later use. But you realise one thing, that 3 hours is long enough to not rush it for most of the recipes. And also there are many repeating elements in recipes, so you can get it done at a time in bulk and store it for future use.

But not all parts are as easy as marinating the chicken though. Some require specific instruments and conditions to work. You can't make Biryani in the oven (Seems we humans are great at defying odds, but anyways my point stands). It requires you to use a sealed pot on a stove-top.

You toil through all of the steps and by night, you have prepared your "Ultra Pro Max Chicken Biryani" to completion and the family celebrates the coming of the new MasterChef in the house!!! While, grandma sitting in the corner raises a glass to you.

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

Ribosomes are molecular chefs that read mRNA recipes and assemble amino acid ingredients into functional proteins.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Shopkeeper understanding your slangs represents ribosomes - the protein-making machinery that reads mRNA
  • Getting ingredients from the list represents translation - converting mRNA code into amino acid sequence
  • Specific cooking equipment needed represents chaperone proteins - helpers for proper protein folding
  • Repeating elements represent subunits of proteins - proteins generally are made up of multi subunits

Key Process: Ribosomes slide along mRNA like reading a list, grabbing the correct amino acids (delivered by tRNA molecules) and linking them together. For complex proteins, chaperone proteins provide the right environment for proper 3D folding. Complex proteins are made of multiple subunits, mostly repeating ones.

Fun Fact: A single ribosome can make a protein in about 1 minute, and cells can have millions of ribosomes working simultaneously!

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

Just like cooking mistakes, genetic errors can occur during DNA copying, transcription, or translation. These are just 2 examples, there are many others as well.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Forgetting sugar in recipe copy represents transcription errors - mistakes when copying DNA to mRNA
  • Shopkeeper's baking powder mix-up represents translation errors - incorrect amino acids during protein synthesis

Key Process: Cells have multiple proofreading mechanisms, like DNA polymerase checking its work during replication, but errors still slip through at rates of about 1 in 10 billion base pairs. Most errors are harmless, but some can have catastrophic consequences.

Fun Fact: Your cells make about 3 trillion DNA copying errors every day, but sophisticated repair systems catch and fix 99.9% of them!

🏆 Entering MasterChef (Plasmids and Horizontal Gene Transfer)

After the passing of your grandma, it was you with whom she entrusted her cookbook with and you promise her to continue the legacy. Now that you have established yourself as a great cook, your family secretly fills your application to enter MasterChef. Under pressure of proving that you are not just some amateur "home cook", but a "Masterchef", you go in search of exploring other cuisines because in a contest you can't just be making one cuisine recipes. You have to be fluent in cuisines from around the world.

So, you befriend Giovanni, who is from Italy, another culinary rich country. He also had a similar origin story as you. He has also inherited a cookbook from his Nonna, and would love to share parts of it with you.

So, you hatch out a plan to have him scan the copy of the book and send it over to you and also send some over to him of your grandma's secret recipe. So, now you have a copy of some of the recipes of Giovanni's Nonna's original cookbook (Yipee!!). But still, you weren't able to win the whole thing, but, hey, its the effort that counts, remember that.

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

Bacteria can share genetic recipes directly with each other, like chefs trading secret techniques, allowing rapid spread of new capabilities.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Trading cookbook sections with Giovanni represents horizontal gene transfer - direct sharing of genetic material
  • Scanning and sending recipes represents plasmid transfer - small DNA circles that move between bacteria
  • Learning cuisines for MasterChef represents acquiring new metabolic abilities - bacteria gaining antibiotic resistance or new functions

Key Process: Unlike vertical inheritance (parent to offspring), bacteria can directly transfer plasmids containing useful genes to neighbouring bacteria through conjugation, transformation, or transduction, spreading beneficial traits rapidly through populations. This is not the case with higher complexity organisms though (just imagine if you could take or give a trait to someone!!)

Fun Fact: This is how antibiotic resistance spreads so quickly - bacteria literally share resistance recipes with each other!

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

Scientists can now deliberately modify genetic recipes to create improved organisms, just like a chef perfecting traditional recipes with modern techniques.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Tweaking ingredients and improving flavors represents directed mutagenesis - making specific genetic changes
  • Pickle Pie represents successful genetic modifications - unexpected combinations that work beautifully
  • Peanut butter cheese represents failed experiments - genetic modifications that don't work

Key Process: Modern tools like CRISPR allow scientists to make precise cuts in DNA and insert new genetic sequences, creating organisms with enhanced or novel capabilities like bacteria that produce insulin or crops resistant to disease.

Fun Fact: We can now edit genes with the precision of a word processor - cutting, copying, pasting, and replacing specific DNA sequences!

🕵️‍♂️ A Thieve arrives (Reverse Transcriptase)

Not resting on your culinary laurels, you continued experimenting with Grandma's recipes. Her sacred "cookbook" – pages slotting neatly into plastic sleeves, held together with metal rings. Easy to organise… and easy to tamper with.

For days, someone out there had been watching you.
From across the street.
From the shadows near your kitchen window.
They noted your habits – when you cooked, how the binder looked and when you left the cookbook unguarded.

Then one evening, it happened.
You had just finished writing out a fresh recipe copy for your "Ultra Pro Max Chicken Biryani" and left it beside the binder as you dashed out to grab some missing spices.

The stalker seized the moment. Silently slipping inside, they snapped a perfect photo of the intact recipe copy – every acronym, every quirky shorthand, all perfectly captured. But they didn't leave just yet. The binder, resting quietly on the counter, was too inviting.

In one smooth move, they unclipped the rings and slid in a few of their own pages – handwritten in a style eerily close to Grandma's. The tea stains, the faded ink, even the little flour smudges – all replicated perfectly. Then they closed it, leaving the binder looking completely untouched.

From that day forward, when you cooked from what you thought was Grandma's flawless legacy, you were also following instructions the thief had planted. And somewhere far away, they smiled cunningly, knowing their recipes were now woven into your kitchen forever.

🧬 Biological Connection

▼

The Kitchen Connection:

Some viruses can reverse the normal genetic flow by converting their RNA back into DNA and inserting it permanently into the host's genetic cookbook.

Story Elements → Biology Mapping:

  • Photographing your recipe copy represents reverse transcription - RNA viruses making DNA copies of themselves
  • Inserting fake pages into the binder represents viral integration - viral DNA becoming part of host genome
  • Perfect style mimicry represents retroviral adaptation - viruses that perfectly mimic host genetic elements
  • Permanent contamination represents provirus formation - viral DNA that becomes permanent part of host genetics
  • Stalker behavior represents viral persistence - how retroviruses establish long-term infections

Key Process: Retroviruses like HIV carry reverse transcriptase enzyme that converts viral RNA into DNA, which then integrates into the host cell's chromosome. This allows the virus to hijack the cell's protein-making machinery permanently.

Fun Fact: About 8% of the human genome consists of ancient retroviral DNA that integrated into our ancestors' cells millions of years ago!
