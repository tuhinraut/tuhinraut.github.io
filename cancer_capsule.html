Cancer Cure in a Capsule: Engineering Bacteria to Deliver Therapeutic miRNAs

Our iGEM journey to develop E. coli Nissle 1917 as a living drug delivery system for colorectal cancer treatment

Published on January 20, 2024 • 15 min read • iGEM 2024 Project
synthetic biology cancer therapy miRNA E. coli Nissle OMVs iGEM

What if the future of cancer treatment could be delivered in a simple capsule containing engineered bacteria? This was the ambitious question that drove our iGEM 2024 project—an attempt to transform E. coli Nissle 1917 into a sophisticated biological factory capable of producing and delivering therapeutic microRNAs directly to cancer cells.

The Problem: Cancer's Molecular Hijacking

Colorectal cancer represents one of the most devastating diseases of our time, ranking as the third most commonly diagnosed cancer globally. But what makes this disease particularly insidious isn't just its prevalence—it's the molecular chaos it creates within our cells.

The MicroRNA Disruption

Cancer cells don't just grow uncontrollably; they systematically disrupt the cellular communication network. MicroRNAs (miRNAs)—tiny molecular regulators that normally keep cell growth in check—become dysregulated. Specifically, tumor suppressor miRNAs like miR-145 are dramatically downregulated, while oncogenic miRNAs are overexpressed. It's like having the cellular brakes fail while the accelerator gets stuck.

Current Treatment Limitations

Traditional cancer therapies—surgery, chemotherapy, radiation—are essentially sledgehammers trying to perform microsurgery. They damage healthy tissue alongside cancerous cells, leading to severe side effects and often incomplete treatment outcomes.

  • Surgery: Risk of complications, infections, and incomplete removal
  • Chemotherapy: Systemic toxicity, hair loss, nausea, immune suppression
  • Radiation: Damage to surrounding healthy tissues
  • Targeted Therapy: High cost, limited patient response, long-term side effects

Our Approach: Living Drug Factories

What if we could engineer bacteria to become living, intelligent drug delivery systems? Our solution centers on transforming E. coli Nissle 1917 (EcN)—a probiotic bacterium already naturally present in the human gut—into sophisticated therapeutic agents.

Why E. coli Nissle 1917?

EcN isn't your typical E. coli. It's a GRAS (Generally Regarded As Safe) strain that's been used as a probiotic for over a century. It naturally colonizes the gut, has an excellent safety profile, and can be engineered without the typical concerns about pathogenicity.

The Four-Component System

1. Novel Chassis Development

We engineered EcN to overproduce Outer Membrane Vesicles (OMVs) by knocking out the tolA gene. These nanoscale spherical vesicles serve as natural delivery vehicles that can transport therapeutic cargo directly to target cells.

2. Therapeutic miRNA Production

Our bacteria produce miR-145, a tumor suppressor microRNA that's significantly downregulated in colorectal cancer. By restoring this crucial regulatory molecule, we aim to reestablish normal cellular growth control.

3. Recombinant RNA-Binding Proteins

We developed designer PPR (Pentatricopeptide Repeat) proteins that specifically bind to our therapeutic miRNAs, ensuring efficient packaging and delivery within the OMVs. Each PPR subunit can be engineered for specific nucleotide recognition.

4. Surface-Displayed Targeting

OMVs are decorated with nanobodies specific to EGFR (Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor), which is overexpressed in CRC cells. This provides targeted delivery, ensuring therapeutic miRNAs reach cancer cells preferentially.

The Science Behind the System

Outer Membrane Vesicles: Nature's Delivery System

OMVs are essentially bacterial "care packages"—naturally occurring nanoparticles that bacteria use for intercellular communication. By knocking out the tolA gene, we dramatically increase OMV production, creating more vehicles for therapeutic delivery.

tolA Knockout Strategy:
1. CRISPR-Cas9 targeting of tolA gene
2. Lambda-Red recombination for precise deletion
3. Verification through SDS-PAGE and Western blot

Designer RNA-Binding Proteins

Our PPR proteins represent a breakthrough in RNA recognition technology. Each 35-residue PPR motif can be programmed to recognize specific nucleotides:

  • PPR2.0_A: Recognizes adenine (A)
  • PPR2.0_C: Recognizes cytosine (C)
  • PPR2.0_G: Recognizes guanine (G)
  • PPR2.0_U: Recognizes uracil (U)

Modular Design Advantage

By linking specific PPR motifs through Golden Gate Assembly, we can create proteins that recognize any desired RNA sequence with high specificity—like building words with molecular letters.

Cre-Lox Regulation System

To ensure safety and control, we implemented a sophisticated genetic switch system:

  1. Bile Activation: When bacteria reach the gut, bile acids trigger Cre recombinase expression, initiating miRNA production
  2. Tetracycline Termination: At therapy completion, tetracycline administration triggers a second Cre-Lox event, permanently shutting down the system

Challenges and Limitations

Technical Hurdles

  • Delivery Efficiency: Ensuring sufficient miRNA reaches target cells in therapeutic concentrations
  • Specificity Concerns: Preventing off-target effects in healthy gut bacteria
  • Stability Issues: Maintaining miRNA integrity during storage and transit
  • Immune Response: Managing potential immunogenicity of engineered OMVs

Regulatory Complexity

Perhaps the greatest challenge lies not in the science, but in the regulatory pathway. Engineered live bacteria as therapeutics represent a new frontier that doesn't fit neatly into existing FDA categories. The approval process would likely require:

  • Extensive safety studies in multiple animal models
  • Containment and biodistribution studies
  • Long-term effects monitoring
  • Novel clinical trial designs

The Broader Vision

Beyond Colorectal Cancer

While our project focused on CRC, the platform technology could potentially address multiple diseases by simply swapping the therapeutic cargo:

Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Deliver anti-inflammatory cytokines directly to inflamed gut tissue

Metabolic Disorders

Provide enzyme replacement therapy for genetic deficiencies

Infectious Diseases

Deploy antimicrobial peptides against resistant pathogens

Autoimmune Conditions

Deliver regulatory molecules to modulate immune responses

The Road Ahead

While our iGEM project timeline concluded, the potential for engineered probiotic therapeutics remains enormous. Current research directions that could build upon our work include:

  • Improved OMV engineering for enhanced targeting
  • Advanced RNA delivery systems with better stability
  • Refined genetic circuits for precise control
  • Comprehensive safety assessment protocols

Industry Momentum

The field of engineered living medicines is rapidly advancing, with companies like Synlogic, Seres Therapeutics, and others developing probiotic-based therapeutics. Our project contributes to this growing foundation of knowledge.

Future Prospects

The convergence of synthetic biology, personalized medicine, and advanced manufacturing could make engineered probiotic therapeutics a reality within the next decade. The question isn't if, but when—and how we can ensure these powerful tools are developed safely and equitably.

Reflections on Failure and Learning

Perhaps the most valuable aspect of this project wasn't the technical achievements, but the lessons learned about scientific ambition, realistic goal-setting, and the iterative nature of discovery. In science, "failure" often provides more learning opportunities than success.

"The beauty of synthetic biology lies not just in what we can build, but in what we learn about life's fundamental principles through the process of trying to engineer them."

Our cancer cure in a capsule remains unrealized—for now. But the foundation we built, the methods we developed, and the insights we gained contribute to a growing body of knowledge that will ultimately make such therapies possible.

What do you think about the potential of engineered bacteria as living medicines? Are there aspects of this approach that excite or concern you? The future of medicine may well depend on our ability to thoughtfully navigate these possibilities.
