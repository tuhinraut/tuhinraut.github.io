Probiotic E. coli Nissle 1917 as Producer and Carrier of miRNA Therapy to Colorectal Cancer Cells

A Novel Gene Therapy Approach for Colorectal Cancer Treatment

This project idea was developed with valuable contributions and insights from Amogh Varshney and Prathamdeep Dhanoa.

Published on August 14, 2025 • 20 min read
gene therapy cancer probiotics

I am sure you would have first hand experience of some family member being diagnosed with the dreaded disease, Cancer. Watching them struggle through the different therapies and medicines the doctor administered to fight off our own rogue cells must have been heart breaking. But, there are some alternate therapies emerging every single day to combat this, of which the most promising one to date is "Gene Therapy".

The following is our attempt as a team to create a "simple" capsule that could fight off this devil incarnate. We had tried our level best to come up with a fool proof solution, but is it ever possible to achieve this in the biological world?

Why Focus on Colorectal Cancer?

Colorectal cancer represents one of the most pressing global health challenges, demanding innovative therapeutic approaches that can address both the scale of the problem and the limitations of current treatments. Our focus on CRC for this project was driven by compelling epidemiological data and significant unmet clinical needs.

The Problem: Limitations of Current Cancer Therapies

Current Cancer Treatment Challenges

Existing therapies for colorectal cancer (CRC) including surgery, chemotherapy, radiation therapy, targeted therapy, and immunotherapy face significant limitations. 4 These treatments often result in severe side effects, limited specificity, high costs, and variable patient responses, highlighting the urgent need for more targeted and personalized therapeutic approaches.

Gene therapy has emerged as a promising solution by addressing these limitations and offering targeted treatment strategies that can revolutionize cancer care. 5 By manipulating genetic material at the cellular level, gene therapy provides the potential for more precise, personalized treatments with reduced collateral damage to healthy tissues. But, it is not perfect either. Existing methods require repeated doses and not a one-time solution. They require continuous monitoring and calculated future dosage schedules .

Our Solution: Engineered Probiotic Delivery System

Figure 1: Project Overview

We aim to solve the problem via continuous highly targeted and optimised delivery of gene therapy products to tumor cells of the gut via probiotic bacteria (which is introduced just once ). This is achieved via in-vivo production of therapeutics (with enhanced bio-availability) by the probiotic bacteria .

Why E. coli Nissle 1917 as our chassis?

E. coli Nissle 1917 (EcN) represents an ideal chassis for this therapeutic approach. As a probiotic bacteria already part of the gut microbiome, EcN is classified as GRAS (Generally Regarded As Safe) by regulatory agencies. 6 This existing safety profile provides a significant advantage for clinical translation compared to other bacterial delivery systems.

Outer Membrane Vesicles (OMVs) as Delivery Vehicles

Our approach leverages Outer Membrane Vesicles (OMVs), nanoscale (0.1-0.2 micron) proteo-liposomes naturally released from Gram-negative bacteria. 7 These vesicles can be engineered to display specific targeting antibodies—in our case, nanobodies—making them highly effective for targeted drug delivery applications.

OMV Engineering

OMVs can be designed to carry therapeutic miRNA cargo while maintaining their natural ability to traverse biological barriers and target specific cell types through surface-displayed nanobodies.

Safety Profile

The use of probiotic bacteria as the production host ensures biocompatibility and reduces the risk of adverse immune reactions compared to synthetic delivery systems.

Figure 3: OMV Structure

miRNA Therapy for Colorectal Cancer

Our therapeutic strategy focuses on delivering miR-145, a tumor suppressor miRNA that is significantly downregulated in colorectal cancer cells. 8 The miR-145 gene spans approximately 1.8 kB and requires additional regulatory sequences for optimal expression and targeting.

miR-145 Targeting Strategy

miR-145 functions as a critical tumor suppressor by regulating multiple oncogenic pathways in colorectal cancer. 9 Its restoration through our proposed probiotic delivery system could potentially have reversed malignant phenotypes and sensitize cancer cells to other therapeutic interventions.

How We Package miR-145 for Delivery through OMV

Getting the therapeutic miRNA from inside the bacteria to the target cancer cells requires a sophisticated packaging and transport system. We need to ensure the miRNA reaches its destination, Outer Membrane Region, intact and functional.

RNA Capture System

Once miR-145 is produced inside our engineered bacteria, we need to "grab" it specifically. We use special proteins called PPR proteins (think of them as highly selective molecular tweezers) that are programmed to recognize and bind only to their recognition site, that we engineered on to our the miR-145 10.

Cellular Delivery System

After capturing miR-145, we need to move it to the right location for packaging into OMVs. We attach a molecular address tag (called PelB signal peptide) that tells the bacterial cell machinery "deliver this package to the outer membrane area" 11, like putting a ZIP code on a package. The cell's transport system recognizes this tag and automatically shuttles our miRNA-protein complex to where the OMVs are formed.

Technical Specifications for miR-145 Processing

PPR Subunit Length: 35 residues per subunit
PelB Signal Sequence: 22 residues (MKYLLPTAAAGLLLLAAQPAMA)
miR-145 Recognition Sequence: 6-8 nucleotides for specific binding
Transport Mechanism: Sec translocon-mediated periplasmic translocation

Surface Nanobody Display for Targeting

To enhance the specificity of our OMV-based therapy, we plan to incorporate surface-displayed nanobodies targeting EGFR (Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor), which is overexpressed in many colorectal cancer cells. 12 VEGFR1-targeting nanobodies have demonstrated high binding affinity and therapeutic efficacy in preclinical studies.

Gene Regulation and Control Systems

Cre-Lox Recombinase System

Our design incorporates sophisticated genetic control mechanisms using the Cre-Lox recombinase system for temporal regulation of miRNA production. 13 This system would enable precise control over therapeutic gene expression, allowing for both initiation and termination of treatment as needed.

Initiation Control

Bile-responsive promoters would activate Cre recombinase upon bacterial colonization of the gut, removing stop sequences and initiating miRNA production in a location-specific manner.

Termination Control

Tetracycline-inducible systems would allow for irreversible shutdown of miRNA production, providing a safety mechanism to terminate therapy when cancer was eliminated.

Figure 8: Cre-Lox Circuit

Chassis Development and Safety

Our approach requires systematic modification of the E. coli Nissle chassis to optimize OMV production while maintaining safety. The engineering strategy involves two critical steps: first, CRISPR-Cas9-mediated removal of native plasmids (pMUT1 and pMUT2), followed by knockout of tolA gene to enhance OMV release, and finally the introduction of our engineered cryptic plasmids containing therapeutic components. 14

Native Plasmid Curing Strategy

E. coli Nissle naturally harbors pMUT1 and pMUT2 cryptic plasmids constitutively. Before proceeding with therapeutic modifications, we needed to cure EcN of these native plasmids to introduce our own modified versions containing therapeutic elements. The CRISPR-Cas9 based curing process utilized two specialized plasmids: pFREE 15 and pCryptDel4.8 16, enabling precise removal of native genetic elements.

tolA Knockout

Many Bacteria natively produce OMVs (which includes EcN). But for drug delivery, we require a larger number of it. tolA gene K/O in E. coli have been shown to increase the OMV production in E. coli 24. For this we need to construct the sgRNA containing plasmid and the Cas9 Plasmid. Substituting the gene with lambda-Red would help us in isolating modified organisms.

The engineered pMUT2 plasmid (pMUT2-pTcpP-mCherry used as base) 17 incorporates regulatory control systems:

  • lox-frt-miRNA System

Modified pMUT1 Engineering

Our engineered version of pMUT1 (pM1s3AsG used as base)18, is designed to carry two critical therapeutic components:

  • Recombinant PPR-PelB Complex
  • Surface Nanobodies

Cryptic Plasmid Advantages

Our choice of cryptic plasmids as the engineering foundation provides several key advantages for therapeutic applications:

  • Antibiotic-Resistance-Free: Eliminates selection pressure reducing regulatory concerns
  • Probiotic Compatible: Maintains the beneficial properties of E. coli Nissle
  • Stable Maintenance: Retained for up to 90 generations without external pressure, ensuring therapeutic consistency 19

Delivery Mechanism and Clinical Application

The delivery of engineered probiotics to patients can be achieved through established encapsulation technologies that protect bacteria during transit through the acidic stomach environment. Various capsule-based delivery systems had been specifically validated for E. coli Nissle 1917, providing a clear pathway for clinical implementation. 20

Technical Challenges and Future Directions

While our engineered probiotic delivery system represents a promising therapeutic approach, several significant technical and biological challenges must be addressed before clinical implementation. These challenges span from fundamental molecular mechanisms to complex ecological interactions within the human gut microbiome.

1. Microbiome Dysbiosis

Introducing engineered ECN could disrupt the established gut microbiome equilibrium, triggering unpredictable cascading effects. Changes in metabolite production, microbial cross-feeding networks, and potential pathogenic niches remain major concerns with unknown long-term consequences.

2. Off-Target miRNA Effects*

Therapeutic miRNAs may be uptaken by healthy intestinal epithelial cells due to OMV uptake promiscuity. This could cause various problems in normal tissues, particularly problematic given high intestinal turnover rates.

3. RNA Packaging Efficiency

The efficiency of miRNA packaging into OMVs and transport to the outer membrane remains unpredictable. Limited understanding of bacterial RNA sorting mechanisms makes optimization difficult, potentially requiring higher bacterial loads that exacerbate safety concerns.

4. Immunogenicity Risks

Despite ECN's safety profile, engineered OMVs and recombinant proteins may trigger immune responses. The gut immune system could recognize modified components as foreign, leading to clearance, inflammation, or neutralizing antibodies that compromise efficacy.

* The off-target miRNA effects can potentially be mitigated through quorum sensing mechanisms that control miRNA production based on bacterial population density. However, implementing such a system would introduce additional complexity to the project, requiring precise threshold controls, managing signal degradation in harsh intestinal conditions, avoiding interference from endogenous bacterial systems, and maintaining genetic circuit stability over time.

The Path Forward

While our probiotic E. coli miRNA delivery approach faced significant challenges, recent breakthroughs in miRNA replacement therapy for colorectal cancer have validated our core concept and demonstrated the therapeutic potential of increasing miR-145 content in cancer cells.

Direct miR-145 Replacement Therapy Success

Preclinical studies have demonstrated that PEI-mediated miR-145 replacement therapy achieved more than 10-fold increases in intracellular miR-145 levels in colon carcinoma cells, resulting in over 60% reduction in cell proliferation 21. In vivo studies showed that miR-145 administration decreased cancer stem cell markers by 45% and increased differentiation markers by over 4000%, validating our therapeutic target 22.

Advanced Delivery System Evolution

While early miRNA replacement trials like MRX34 faced challenges, newer TargomiR technologies using bacterially-derived minicells with targeting antibodies have shown encouraging clinical results 23. These targeted delivery vehicles could be integrated with our probiotic approach to achieve precise tumor cell targeting while avoiding systemic toxicity.

The Story's End

We had notebooks filled with calculations, plasmid designs sketched on whiteboards, and dreams of engineering bacteria to fight cancer. The idea felt within reach—take a harmless probiotic, teach it to make medicine, and let it work its magic right where tumors grow. Sure, the technical challenges were huge, but that's what made it exciting. We were ready to dive into months of lab work, cloning genes, testing systems, and slowly building this microscopic pharmacy. Sometimes the best projects are the ones that never leave the drawing board, not because they weren't good enough, but because life had other plans.
